The Failed Generation

There was once an orphan boy who toiled as a stable hand, knowing no mother or father. His only true companion was a rooster, blind in both its eyes, which followed him everywhere. The rooster often pecked at empty air, or tripped over buckets, and caused much bother, though it never truly meant harm. The stable master was a harsh man, quick with his switch, and often grew wroth at the rooster's blunders. One morning, the blind rooster upset a pail of fresh milk, and it was the boy who received the beating for it. Still, the boy always shared his meager crust of bread with the blind rooster and never blamed the creature. So the days passed, each one much like the last, filled with mucking out stalls and little food. Sometimes, the boy would see the King's castle on a high hill, and it shone like a star, far from the grime of the stables. He never dreamed he might one day see its gates, for such was not the lot of a stable boy. And the blind rooster pecked faithfully at his heels, clucking softly.
It came to pass that the King of that land declared a great task to be done, and a reward for any who could achieve it. A precious Sunstone, a jewel of great power, had been lost on the Dragon's Crag, and he who returned it would be greatly honored. Many knights and heroes of the realm declared they would venture forth, eager for glory and gold. The orphan boy heard the whispers in the stables, but it was not for him to consider such a grand undertaking. But as a proud knight was preparing his steed, the blind rooster, pecking about for stray oats, swallowed the knight's lucky fighting-feather. The knight, seeing this, roared in fury, crying, 'That bird has eaten my fortune, and you, boy, shall pay for it!' The stable master, ever eager to please the knight, declared, 'We shall wring its neck and search its gullet for the feather, sire!' Then the orphan boy, trembling but resolute, stepped forth and said, 'Spare the rooster, and I shall fetch the Sunstone from the Dragon's Crag.' The knight and the stable master looked at him, then burst into loud laughter, saying, 'You, a stable scrub, achieve what heroes dare not?' Yet the knight, with a sneer, agreed, 'So be it, fetch the Sunstone by the next full moon, or you and that cursed bird shall both be for the pot.'
And so the orphan boy left the stables with no sword, no shield, and only the blind rooster stumbling behind him. Before they had even left the shadow of the castle walls, the rooster, thinking it a large, soft grub, pecked a hole in the boy's only waterskin. The boy sighed, but patched it clumsily with a broad leaf, and trudged onward. They journeyed for many days across barren plains where the sun beat down, and then into stony hills. Their few crusts of bread grew fewer, and the boy's stomach often rumbled with emptiness. One evening, as hunger gnawed fiercely, the blind rooster began to cluck triumphantly, having stumbled upon a single, plump red berry. The boy, his eyes wide with need, reached for it, intending to share it with his companion. But just as his fingers brushed it, an old woman with eyes like winter frost appeared from behind a rock and croaked, 'Hold, child! That berry is Death's own morsel.' The boy drew back his hand as if burned, his heart thumping a sudden fear. 'Aye,' the old woman rasped, 'one bite, and it sucks the soul dry,' while the blind rooster pecked hopefully at the ground beside the lethal fruit.
The old woman then looked keenly at the boy and said, 'If you would truly find your way, first you must gather three sighs from a silent stone.' The boy did not understand how this would help him find the Sunstone, but he nodded slowly. She pressed a single dry oatcake into his hand, saying, 'Sustain yourself,' and then she was gone as if the wind had taken her. The boy, though his own belly ached, broke the oatcake in two and gave the larger piece to the blind rooster. After a time, they came upon a great grey boulder by the wayside, so still and ancient it seemed as if it had held its breath for centuries. The boy remembered the old woman's words and sat down beside the boulder, but he knew not what to do to make a stone sigh. The blind rooster, however, began to peck vigorously at the moss growing on the stone, perhaps mistaking it for a patch of tasty insects. Its sharp beak dislodged a flake of stone, and beneath it, nestled in a tiny hollow, was a tiny, intricately carved wooden bird. The boy picked it up carefully; it was so delicate it seemed a breath could break it. And as he held it, a low, mournful sound, like wind through ancient ruins, seemed to issue from the heart of the great grey boulder; it was the first sigh.
The boy felt a strange sadness touch his own heart, though he knew not why. He placed the wooden bird gently back in its hollow, but as he did so, the blind rooster stumbled against him, knocking his elbow hard against the boulder. This time, a different sound, a faint chink, was heard, and a small, rusted iron key fell from a crevice he had not seen. The boy picked up the key and turned it over in his palm; it was cold and heavy, and clearly meant to open some forgotten lock. As his fingers closed around it, a grating sound, like old hinges groaning, echoed from within the stone; this was the second sigh. Now the boy felt he must find the third sigh, though he still knew not how. The blind rooster, chasing a beetle only it could hear, ran full tilt into the great grey boulder with a loud thwack, then reeled back, squawking indignantly. At the force of this blow, a thin crack snaked across the boulder's face, and from it, nothing fell, but it revealed an empty hollow within. And from this crack, and the emptiness within, came a whisper so faint it was barely sound at all, like the last breath escaping a dying man; this was the third sigh. The boy knew not what this emptiness meant, but he covered his face with his hands and wept for the sorrow of the silent stone.
When the boy had finished his weeping, the old woman stood before him once more, as suddenly as she had vanished. She nodded, her frosty eyes glinting, and said, 'You have done well, child, to gather three sighs where there was only silence.' Then she gave him a single, iridescent feather from a bird that never was, and told him, 'This will show you true north, even in a liar's heart.' The boy took the feather and thanked her humbly, for he felt its strange power. So he set off once more towards the Dragon's Crag, the feather clutched in his hand, and the blind rooster ambling after. They soon came to a treacherous marsh where paths twisted and lied, but the feather glowed faintly, always pointing the one true way. The blind rooster, paying no heed to the feather, hopped onto what it thought was solid ground and promptly sank up to its wattles in mud. The boy pulled it free with a sigh, cleaned its mucky feathers as best he could, and they continued on. At last, the feather led them to the edge of a forest so dark the sun itself seemed lost above its canopy. The iridescent feather pulsed with a steady light, pointing directly into the shadowy depths, towards the Dragon's Crag.
A shiver went down the boy's spine, but he clutched the glowing feather tightly, took a deep breath, and stepped forward into the gloom. Strange rustlings echoed from unseen places, no birdsong broke the silence, and the air was heavy and cold. Even the blind rooster, for once, was quiet, and trod softly, its head cocked as if listening to the dread in the very soil. The feather in the boy's hand was the only thing that shone, a tiny beacon in the utter darkness that pressed in around them. Soon, they began to see bones scattered amongst the roots of ancient trees ? the bones of men and beasts, picked clean and white. The boy's blood ran cold at the sight, but he thought of the knight's threat and the rooster's fate, and pushed onward. Suddenly, the blind rooster, perhaps forgetting its fear or sensing something only it could, puffed its chest and let out a series of loud, challenging crows. The boy, horrified, quickly clapped his hand over its beak, whispering, 'Hush, you fool, or you'll wake the dead, or worse!' But it was too late; a low rumble, like thunder from beneath the earth, answered the rooster's call from deeper in the forest. The feather in the boy's hand began to shine with an urgent, throbbing light, pulling him faster now, deeper into the woods towards the source of the rumble.
The rumbling grew to a roar, and soon they stood at the foot of a jagged peak of black rock that reeked of sulfur, from which smoke coiled like angry serpents. And there, coiled upon the highest pinnacle, was a vast, scaled creature with eyes like molten gold and teeth like daggers, the Dragon itself. Clutched in one of its massive claws was the Sunstone, which pulsed with a soft, warm light, a stark contrast to the beast's fiery breath. The boy's heart hammered against his ribs, but he knew he could not turn back; he looked for a path, a distraction, any hope. Then, the blind rooster did a most astonishing thing: it puffed its chest again, not with a foolish crow, but with an odd, serene confidence, and began to strut directly towards the sleeping Dragon. The Dragon, disturbed, opened one vast eye, saw the small, blind bird, and the earth-shaking rumble it emitted sounded almost like a purr. The rooster then hopped onto the Dragon's great snout, pecked three times gently on one of its scales, and the fearsome beast, with a sigh of smoke, gently nudged the Sunstone from its claw towards the little bird. The orphan boy stood frozen, his mouth agape, for he could not believe his eyes. The blind rooster carefully picked up the Sunstone in its beak ? it glowed so brightly it illuminated the bird from within ? and then it strutted back to the boy. The great Dragon watched them with its one open eye, then closed it with another smoky sigh, and seemed to fall asleep once more.
Still in a daze, the boy took the glowing Sunstone from the rooster's beak and, turning his back on the sleeping Dragon, began the journey out of the dark forest. The path out of the forest seemed clearer now, the air lighter, and no strange rustlings or bones troubled their passage. The boy walked in silence for a long time, and he looked at the blind rooster with new eyes, wondering at its secret. The rooster, for its part, seemed to have forgotten its strange courage at the Crag, and presently it stumbled over a root and squawked as it always had. After many more days, they left the stony hills and the barren plains behind and saw again the green lands of the kingdom, and the King's castle shining on its hill. The boy's heart was heavy with a mixture of hope and dread, for the next full moon was but two nights away, and he remembered the knight's cruel promise. He clutched the Sunstone, and its warm light seemed to pulse gently against his palm as if to offer courage. As they passed through villages and fields, folk who knew him from the castle stables pointed and stared, for none had expected to see him return. He spoke to no one, his gaze fixed on the distant castle, the Sunstone hidden beneath his ragged shirt. And the blind rooster, unaware of the castle before them or the Sunstone's glow, pecked at the boy's ankles, clucking as if it alone knew the true path forward.
At long last, the orphan boy, ragged and coated in dust, stood before the great gates of the King's castle, the blind rooster at his side. The guards at the gate, clad in shining armor, crossed their spears and said, 'Be off, vagrant! This is no place for the likes of you.' The boy stood his ground and replied, 'I have come as the King decreed; I have returned with the Sunstone.' Hearing this, the guards roared with laughter, one saying to the other, 'Did you hear that? This dust-covered whelp claims the Dragon's prize!' Then the boy drew forth the Sunstone from beneath his shirt, and its light blazed forth with such a blinding radiance that the guards gasped and fell back a step. The commotion and the brilliant light brought the Captain of the Guard, a stern man with a grizzled beard, running to the gate. When the Captain saw the Sunstone in the stable boy's hand, his own eyes widened, and he barked, 'This boy comes with me to the King at once!' So the orphan boy, still clutching the Sunstone, was marched through marble halls and past rich tapestries, the blind rooster hopping confusedly at his heels. The rooster, dazzled by the gleam, suddenly pecked decisively at a golden tassel hanging from a velvet drape, nearly unravelling it. One guard grunted and swatted at the bird, but their attention was fixed on the boy and the King's summons.
They entered the throne room, a vast chamber gleaming with gold and jewels, where the King sat upon a high throne, surrounded by courtiers in silk. The King had a stern face and eyes that missed nothing; he tapped his fingers on the arm of his throne, a sign of his impatience. The Captain of the Guard bowed low and announced, 'Your Majesty, this stable boy, found at the gates, claims to have the Sunstone.' Murmurs of disbelief and a few stifled snickers rippled through the assembled courtiers, their fine clothes rustling. The King's gaze fixed upon the orphan, sharp and cold. 'A bold claim for a tender of horses,' he said. 'Show it then, if you dare speak truth in our presence.' With a trembling hand, the boy reached into his ragged shirt and drew forth the Sunstone. A light erupted from his small hand, so pure and warm it filled every corner of the vast throne room, chasing away all shadows, and a collective gasp of awe arose from all present. The King himself leaned forward on his throne, his stern face softened by wonder, his eyes fixed on the glowing jewel. At this very moment of hushed awe, the blind rooster, perhaps finding a stray mote of dust in the Sunstone's light, began to preen its feathers loudly and contentedly. After a long silence, the King spoke, his voice thick with an unknown emotion, 'It is indeed the Sunstone, returned to us at last.'
The King then fixed his gaze, no longer cold but filled with a burning curiosity, upon the orphan boy and asked, 'Tell us, child, how did you, a mere tender of horses, succeed where our bravest knights have failed?' The boy, still overawed, began his tale, but spoke little of his own hardships, and much of the blind rooster and its strange encounter with the Dragon. At the telling of the rooster's deed, fresh murmurs, this time of outright disbelief, went through the court; a rooster subduing a Dragon was beyond imagining. The proud knight whose fighting-feather had been eaten then stepped forward, his face red with anger, and declared, 'Your Majesty, this is surely nonsense, a fabrication! He speaks lies or witchcraft to mock us all!' But the King, gazing at the Sunstone's pure light, then at the boy's earnest face, and finally at the preening rooster, raised a hand for silence and asked the boy, 'This blind rooster of yours... is it always so bold, or so peculiar in its actions?' The boy shook his head. 'Nay, Your Majesty,' he said, 'mostly he is a menace to pails of milk and a terror to stray grubs, and oft trips over his own feet.' As if to prove the boy's words, the blind rooster then hopped onto the steps of the throne and gave a curious peck to the King's gleaming golden Sceptre lying beside it. The King gasped, his eyes widening not with anger, but with a dawning, terrible understanding, and he gripped the arms of his throne until his knuckles were white. Then, in a voice barely a whisper, the King spoke, not to the boy, but to the bird, 'Cluck-a-Din... my old... is it truly you, returned in this guise?' The blind rooster, hearing the King's words, ceased its preening, stood very still, and then, deliberately, bowed its head thrice before the throne.
An utter silence fell upon the throne room; every courtier held his breath, staring from the King to the stable boy, and then to the blind rooster. The King, his voice still trembling, said, 'There is a story here, one I had thought but a child's nightmare, or a madman's jest.' He spoke of a time long ago, when 'Cluck-a-Din' was not blind, but a magnificent creature, the Guardian of the Royal Hatchlings, possessor of great wisdom. A wicked sorcerer, who coveted the Sunstone even then, had laid a cruel curse upon him, stealing his sight and his golden voice, for Cluck-a-Din had tried to protect the jewel. In the ensuing chaos, the Sunstone was lost to the Dragon's Crag, and Cluck-a-Din, broken and shamed, vanished from the castle, believed by all to have perished. The King's voice was heavy with remembered pain. 'It was a sorrow I have borne for many years, believing my old friend lost and the Sunstone beyond recovery.' 'It seems,' the King continued, his gaze soft upon the rooster, 'that some ancient loyalty, or a dim memory of its duty, led it to you, boy, and then to the Dragon, which perhaps remembered it too.' The proud knight who had demanded the rooster's death now hung his head, his face, once red with anger, now pale with shame and awe. 'And you, orphan boy,' said the King, his voice now firm, 'though you knew it not, have been an instrument of a fate far older than yourself, and have undone a great evil by your simple companionship.' He then declared, 'For this, you shall be rewarded as befits such a service to the crown and to my own heart, but first, your worth must be further known.'
The King, stroking the now-alert Cluck-a-Din who had hopped upon his knee, proclaimed, 'For your first task, you must bring to me by the morrow's eve a cloak woven from the whispers of the wind.' The orphan boy's heart sank, for how could any mortal man weave a cloak from mere whispers on the breeze? But Cluck-a-Din, perched on the King's armrest, let out a soft, strangely melodious cluck and pecked meaningfully towards an open window. The boy, trusting the rooster more than his own despair, went to the window and looked out upon the castle gardens. The moon had risen, and in its silvery light, he saw that countless spiders were spinning intricate webs between the rose bushes, shimmering like captured frost. A thought, light as a moth's wing, entered his mind, perhaps from Cluck-a-Din's soft clucking, that these near-invisible threads were the very whispers made visible. All through the night, the boy worked with infinite care, his fingers deft and gentle, gathering the shimmering strands of silk from the webs without breaking them. Cluck-a-Din stood solemnly by, occasionally pecking at the air as if trying to see the almost invisible threads the boy collected on a frame of twigs. As the first light of dawn touched the castle towers, he had before him a shimmering heap of silk, light as a cloud, and from this, he fashioned a cloak that seemed to capture the very essence of moonlight and morning mist. He carried it carefully back to the throne room, and though it shimmered and flowed like water in his hands, it was so fine it was almost unseen unless the light caught it just so.
When the boy presented the cloak of whispers to the King, a collective sigh of wonder went through the court, for it was delicate yet strong, and seemed to murmur with faint sounds as it moved. The King nodded, his eyes thoughtful, and said, 'You have done well, stable boy. For your second task, you must fetch me a tear from the eye of the Man in the Moon, and return before three nights have passed.' The boy's spirit, which had soared with the completion of the first task, now plummeted to earth, for this was surely a task beyond any hero, let alone him. But Cluck-a-Din, after a moment's thought, ruffled his feathers, stared intently at the highest turret of the castle which pierced the sky, then at the boy, and gave a single, soft coo. The boy, having learned to trust the rooster's strange ways, excused himself from the King and made his way to that very turret, Cluck-a-Din following. It was a disused chamber at the very top, an old, forgotten study, filled with charts of the stars and a single, enormous telescope wrought of brass, pointed towards the heavens. The boy gazed at the telescope, and then at Cluck-a-Din, and he understood that he might not need to journey to the moon, but perhaps bring the moon's sorrow to him through this strange eye of glass. That night, under a clear, dark sky, the boy pointed the great telescope towards the full moon, and after much careful adjustment, he saw the Man in the Moon, and his face was etched with a loneliness as old as time, and a single, silver tear was rolling down his cheek. And as the boy watched, holding his breath, that very tear detached itself from the moon-man's cheek, and like a tiny falling star, it sped through the darkness and landed upon the telescope lens, a shimmering dewdrop of pure starlight. With trembling fingers, the boy touched a sliver of glass to the lens, and the moon-tear clung to it, cool to the touch, yet pulsed with a gentle warmth; he had his prize.
On the second morning, the orphan boy stood before the King once more and presented, upon the sliver of glass, a single drop of liquid light that shone with all the moon's soft sorrow. The King gazed upon the moon-tear, and a great sigh escaped him, a sigh that echoed the stone's sorrow from days before, and for a moment, his own eyes grew moist. 'Twice you have succeeded where all others would fail,' the King said, his voice low. 'For your third and final task, you must take this cursed coin,' and he held out a black, heavy piece of metal, 'and by it, make the Devil himself weep with remorse for one of his wicked deeds.' A dread colder than the forest's gloom filled the boy, for to seek out the Devil and try to stir his heart was a path to certain torment or damnation. Cluck-a-Din, however, did not coo or peck, but with a sudden beat of its wings, it flew ? for the first time the boy had ever seen it truly fly ? and landed on the high sill of the throne room's chapel window, looking upwards. The boy understood. He took the cold, cursed coin from the King, and knew he must seek counsel not of the earth but of Heaven for such a quest. He went to the castle chapel, a quiet place of stone and coloured glass, and knelt before the altar, the cursed coin heavy in his hand. He prayed not for his own safety, but for understanding, and as he did, a single beam of sunlight broke through the high window and struck the coin, which grew hot in his hand and began to weep tears of black, oily sorrow. And as it wept, the boy saw in his mind the story of the coin: it was the price a desperate mother had been paid by a shadowy stranger to sell her own child's laughter for a moment's warmth in a harsh winter, a bargain struck in deepest despair. The boy's own eyes filled with tears for that lost laughter and that mother's despair, and he knew that with this weeping coin, he must now find the Devil at the crossroads of midnight and despair.
Clutching the sorrowing coin and the iridescent feather, the boy went to a desolate crossroads where three dead trees stood, and there he waited as the hour of midnight approached. As the last stroke of midnight faded, a cold wind swept the crossroads, and before the boy stood a tall stranger clad in shadows, with eyes that burned like coals and a smile that promised everything and nothing. The Devil's voice was smooth as oiled silk, yet carried an edge of ancient ice. 'Well now,' he purred, 'what brings such a tender morsel to my lonely vigil on this night of nights?' The boy, holding the still-weeping coin aloft so its black tears caught the starlight, did not speak of bargains, but told of the mother, the child's lost laughter, and the bitter coin that sealed such woe. The Devil listened, his fiery eyes narrowed, and then he laughed, a sound like dry leaves skittering over tombstones. 'And this one bauble of misery concerns you, little fool? Such trifles are the bread of my existence.' But the boy only held the coin closer, its black tears now visibly sizzling as they dripped near the Devil's shadowy form, and said softly, 'It was not just any sorrow, Lord of Lies, but a child's laughter that was slain for this black metal.' At the words 'child's laughter,' the Devil's smile faltered for an instant, and a shadow of an ancient memory, swift and terrible, seemed to cross his burning eyes. He recoiled a step, the fiery light in his eyes dimming to a dull, smoky red, and for the first time, a look of genuine distress marred his features. Then, to the boy's utter astonishment, a single, black tear, like congealed night, welled in the Devil's eye and traced a burning path down his shadowy cheek, hissing as it struck the barren earth. With a howl of fury and shame that shook the dead trees, the Devil vanished in a clap of sulfurous thunder, leaving the boy alone with the cursed coin, which now lay strangely peaceful in his palm, its weeping ceased.
The boy made his way back to the castle as dawn was breaking; his face was pale, and his eyes held a new gravity, but in his hand, the once-cursed coin felt light and clean. He stood before the King, who looked older himself, and laid the coin upon the arm of the throne; it no longer wept, but gleamed with a soft, forgiving light. The King touched the coin, then looked at the boy with an awe that surpassed all his earlier wonder. 'You have faced the Unnamable and brought back a whisper of its regret,' he said.
