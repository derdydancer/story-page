The Sundered Crown 100

Once upon a time, in a kingdom blessed with abundant sunshine, life seemed untroubled and prosperous. In this kingdom dwelt a King, known for his fair governance. He had by his side a Queen, whose appearance was noted by many in the court. Together, they had two sons: one known for his thoughtful disposition, the other for his quiet nature. The kingdom flourished under their reign, consistently bathed in a gentle, unwavering light. Yet, though the Queen was admired for her grace, a shadow lay upon her heart that none could perceive or understand. One day, she approached the King and said, "My lord, a strange and persistent shadow chills my spirit." "Prithee, grant me leave to journey across the wide sea, to seek a cure that may set my troubled spirit free." The King's heart grew heavy upon hearing her words, for he held great affection for his Queen, but seeing her visible unhappiness, he granted her request. So the Queen departed from the kingdom by ship, leaving behind her husband and sons, who watched her vessel sail away with troubled expressions.
The Queen journeyed for many weeks across the sea until she came to a desolate shore where a stark, black tower stood against the grey sky. Inside this tower, upon a simple stone seat, sat a tall Sorcerer with watchful, cold eyes. "I know why you have come to this place, Queen," said the Sorcerer, his voice even, "to find a way to overcome the shadow on your heart." "But you must understand that all magic, all such remedies, have a price." "What will you give me, Queen, to feel that troubling shadow on your spirit no more?" The Queen, her desire for relief overriding other concerns, answered quickly, "If you can end this torment that I feel, ask for anything you wish." "I will give my all if this crushing weight will fall from my soul at last!" The Sorcerer replied with a thin smile, "I ask not for your gold or your kingdom's gems." "But the next time you truly cherish a thing of beauty, save for your own kin, a sliver of its perceived joy shall be my fee." "Agree to this, and you will find your spirit's ease from this day forward."
"A sliver of its joy?" Queen Anna repeated, considering his words carefully. "That seems a small price indeed for the peace I seek. I agree! Your terms I shall fulfill without fail!" Then the Sorcerer lifted one hand, and a sudden, sharp chill touched the Queen's brow. At once, she felt a profound wave of peace wash over her, and her eyes, moments before clouded, regained their former light. She turned and departed from the dark tower with a lighter step, but unseen by her, a thin thread of shadow unspooled from her very being, tethering her back to the Sorcerer's domain. The Sorcerer watched her go, and a flicker of something unreadable crossed his face as he thought, "The magic is now spun." "What joy she touches and cherishes from this moment, save for her own kin, will now surely be undone." After some time had passed, Queen Anna returned to the royal palace, looking radiant and peaceful once more. King Peter rejoiced greatly to see her vibrant self apparently restored to its former state. Just then, as if by unfortunate timing, a courtier brought forth a magnificent new crown adorned with many flawless gems, a gift to the royal house signifying unity and joy.
"Behold this incredible treasure, my dear!" said the King with a broad smile, placing the glittering item into Anna's hands. Queen Anna gazed upon the crown, truly admiring its intricate beauty, and cried aloud, "Oh, what wondrous craftsmanship this is!" "It fills my very soul with delight to behold such an object!" At that very instant, as the words of delight left her lips, the crown's many gems visibly dimmed in her hands. A look of sudden fear dawned on Anna's face, and King Peter frowned with a distinct flicker of unease, though the surrounding court noticed little of the change. "The light..." she whispered, her gaze fixed on the dulled stones. "It is as if a breath of its joy was stolen away!" "It is splendid, my love," said the King, though his expression of unease remained fixed. "Perhaps it is merely a trick of the light in this hall." But from that day forward, a subtle shadow began to creep over the kingdom, born from Anna's secret bargain, and the once vibrant hues of joy slowly started to fade from the land. Many years passed; King Peter grew older and visibly more troubled, for the kingdom's bright colours had perceptibly faded, and Queen Anna often walked as if in a constant shade. He felt with a growing certainty that something was deeply wrong and remembered old tales told in his youth of a Magic Mirror hidden in the castle attics that showed truths long past or cleverly concealed.
With a visibly heavy heart, he ascended to the dusty attics, searched among forgotten things, and eventually found a tall, ancient Mirror covered entirely in dark velvet. He pulled the heavy cloth away from its surface and commanded in a low voice, "Mirror, Mirror on the wall, show me the cause of the sorrow we keep within these halls!" Slowly, images formed upon the Mirror's glass, showing him Queen Anna standing before the Sorcerer's dark tower many years before. He saw her striking the fateful bargain for her heart's ease, an ease that would be paid for by a sliver of the kingdom's shared joy. He watched with a frozen expression as the Mirror then showed Anna in the great hall, holding the gifted crown, its brilliant light visibly dimming as she cherished its form. King Peter staggered back from the revealing glass, one hand moving to his chest as if to hold a sudden pain. "Anna..." he whispered to the empty attic air. "For your own ease... you allowed the kingdom to be thus betrayed?" Though filled with an observable grief that shook his frame, a firm resolve slowly formed in his expression. He covered the Mirror once again with the velvet cloth and decided that, for the sake of the kingdom's already fragile peace, this truth must remain hidden from all. He therefore decreed that the Queen's old illness had unfortunately deepened, and she must be confined to distant chambers for her greatly troubled mind and for her own protection.
So it was done as the King had decreed; Queen Anna was moved to rooms in a remote wing of the palace. More years passed, and during this time a vast and menacing Dark Forest of Thorns grew along the kingdom's borders, seemingly dividing the land itself. King Peter continued to rule, but with a perpetually heavy heart; Queen Anna was seldom seen by anyone, and the two Princes grew from boys to young men in this ongoing time of gloom. All who lived in the kingdom felt the undeniable weight of the encroaching shadow; even the ancient trees in the royal parks seemed to sigh with a strange weariness. Indeed, true joy was now a scarce commodity in the increasingly sundered land. After still more years had passed, King Peter, perhaps seeking some measure of warmth for his now grown sons and the quiet court, took a new wife. The new Queen was known as a gentle woman, and she came to the palace with her own young son and a young daughter. This new Queen, whose given name was Petra, tried her best to bring some measure of grace and gentleness to the deeply sorrowful place. The King, in his own quiet way, was grateful for her calming presence in the shadowed halls. One day, some months after her arrival, Queen Petra suggested to the King, "Let the old Royal Preserve at the edge of the castle grounds remain undisturbed, a quiet haven untouched by our daily concerns."
"Let us perhaps proclaim that none shall enter its shaded paths from this day forward." King Peter, weary from many long years of sorrow and now trusting his new wife's gentle judgement, agreed to this without much deliberation. "So be it," he said quietly. The thoughtful elder Prince, whose name was Philip, overheard this new decree and wondered greatly about the now forbidden Royal Preserve, while unseen by him, his mother, the old Queen Anna, watched him from a shadowed alcove. Soon after this new rule was made known throughout the palace, the old Queen Anna summoned Prince Philip to her distant, quiet chambers. "My son," she said, her voice frail but her gaze strangely intense, "you see the many shadows that linger in this house, do you not?" "Things here are not as they seem; your father is weakened by long sorrow, and the new Queen now holds much sway over his decisions." "But," she whispered, leaning even closer to him, "in the Royal Preserve, now so curiously forbidden to all, lies a hidden secret that could restore me fully and reveal the entire truth of our kingdom's sorrow." Prince Philip, who still held great affection for his mother and wished earnestly to ease the kingdom's pervasive sorrow, believed her carefully chosen words. "Mother," he vowed with a determined set to his jaw, "if the secret you speak of truly lies within the Royal Preserve, then I shall seek it out, no matter the King's decree!" A hidden, satisfied smile touched Queen Anna's lips as Philip left her chambers, but the quiet younger Prince, William, who had by chance overheard their exchange from nearby, watched his elder brother go with a visibly troubled heart.
That very night, under the cloak of darkness, Prince Philip went secretly into the forbidden Royal Preserve. The air within its boundaries felt ancient and surprisingly peaceful, quite unlike the sorrowful, shadowed atmosphere of the palace he had just left. He came eventually to a moonlit glade where an ancient, gnarled tree stood beside a quietly bubbling spring, and there an Old Woman of indeterminate age stood as if expecting him. "What troubles you so deeply, Prince," asked she, her voice like the rustling of dry leaves, "that you seek these hidden glades in the dark of night?" "I seek the true reason for the deep sorrow that casts its long shadow over our land and all its people," Philip answered with forthrightness. "This Preserve is indeed a healing place," the Old Woman said slowly. "The only shadows found here are those brought by a troubled heart that is perhaps listening to false tales." "Seek instead the clear reflection that denies all lies; it may show you the unvarnished truth you search for." Then she vanished as silently as mist among the deep shadows of the surrounding trees, leaving Prince Philip alone with her perplexing words. Pondering her strange riddle for a long moment, Philip suddenly remembered an old nurse's tale from his childhood concerning a forgotten truth-telling Mirror hidden away somewhere in the castle attics. He returned to the silent palace, searched the castle attics with renewed purpose, and at last, behind a heavy, moth-eaten tapestry, found a tall glass covered in thick, dusty velvet.
He pulled the heavy velvet cloth away and spoke clearly to the strange, mist-filled glass: "Mirror of Truth, if that is what you are, reveal the story lost to time!" The Mirror's surface swirled with grey mists, and then it showed him his own mother, Queen Anna, as a younger woman, making the terrible bargain with the Sorcerer for her own ease, a bargain that cost the kingdom its joy. It further showed her some time later in the great hall, cherishing the beautiful, gifted crown even as its brilliant light visibly dimmed in her very hands. Philip recoiled from the Mirror as if struck, a sudden coldness spreading through his limbs as he absorbed the truth. "My mother's choice..." he thought, his expression bleak. "Not for the kingdom's sake, as she claimed, but for her own personal gain she caused this endless, pervasive pain!" He covered the Magic Mirror slowly with its velvet cloth once more, a changed young man, burdened by this new and terrible knowledge. With a heavy but resolute expression, Prince Philip went straight from the attics to his mother's secluded chambers. "Mother," he said quietly, his voice devoid of its usual warmth, "I have now seen the hidden truth of the past." "I know of the Sorcerer's tower, the terrible price you paid for your own comfort, and how for your ease, the entire kingdom was unknowingly betrayed." Queen Anna bowed her head low, and visible tears began to fall upon her clasped hands. "My son," she whispered, her voice thick with regret, "it is all true as you say."
"My own selfishness at that time wounded all of you, and indeed, the whole land." "I sowed a truly bitter seed that day, a seed sprung from the deep darkness I felt within my own spirit then." Prince Philip observed her display of remorse, and his expression showed only a deep sorrow, not triumph or anger. He then shared the entirety of this painful truth with his father, King Peter, with his quiet brother, Prince William, and with the gentle Queen Petra. Among them all, a sober understanding slowly grew, and the fractured trust that had long existed between some of them began, ever so slightly, to mend. Though the menacing Dark Forest of Thorns on the kingdom's edge might never fully recede, nor the stolen light completely return to its former brilliance, a quiet and tentative hope nevertheless found some small room to grow in their hearts once more. And perhaps, in the slow unfolding of future time, the sundered kingdom healed as best it could from its long affliction. The quiet Prince William, who had listened intently to all that was shared, kept his own counsel for many days following these events. He observed a slight, almost imperceptible, lessening of the pervasive gloom in the palace halls, a subtle shift in the quality of light.
