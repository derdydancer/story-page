The Sundered crown 200

In a kingdom blessed by the sun, the days were ever bright and the harvests always plentiful. The King, a just ruler, wore a great crown that gleamed with a thousand flawless gems. His Queen, a woman of great beauty and gentle heart, was beloved by all the people. Their shining palace echoed with song and the laughter of children from dawn until dusk. Beyond the palace walls, the fields were ever green and the rivers ran clear and cool. The people of the kingdom lived in contentment, their days untroubled by want or fear. But unseen by all, a shadow began to creep into the Queen's gentle heart. Her laughter, once like silver bells, grew less frequent, and her songs became fainter each day. The King noticed the change in his beloved Queen and grew troubled, though he knew not the cause. The light in her eyes, which once rivaled the palace gems, seemed to dim, and no physician could find a cure.
So the King sent messengers throughout his own lands and beyond, seeking anyone who might restore the Queen's spirit. Many came, wise women and learned men, offering herbs and incantations, but none possessed the art to lift the shadow. Then one evening, as despair settled upon the court, a tall figure, cloaked and hooded, appeared unbidden at the palace gate. The stranger was brought before the King and spoke in a low voice, saying, I can bring solace to the Queen and lift the shadow on her heart. The King, clutching at any hope, asked, What is your price for such a miracle? The cloaked figure looked upon the King with cold eyes and said, A trifle, Your Majesty, hardly worth mentioning before the deed is done. In his eagerness for the Queen's relief, the King agreed to whatever terms the stranger might later name. The Sorcerer was then led to the Queen's chambers, where she lay pale and listless upon her bed. He murmured strange words and made a pass with his hand, seeming to draw a slender, dark thread from the Queen's breast. Instantly, a touch of colour returned to her cheeks, and her eyes brightened with a spark of their former light.
The King, overjoyed at this swift change, thanked the stranger profusely. The Sorcerer bowed low and said, My price is small, Your Majesty: merely a single rose from your gardens, whenever its beauty brings you true joy. The King, much relieved, readily agreed, for his gardens were filled with countless roses of every hue. With another bow, the Sorcerer vanished as silently as he had come, leaving the court to marvel at the Queen's recovery. For many weeks, the Queen returned to her former self; her laughter echoed in the palace, and she took delight in all things beautiful. One morning, the King, his heart light with happiness, walked with her in the royal gardens, where the roses bloomed in profusion. He spied a perfect red rose, its petals like velvet, and as true joy swelled within him at its beauty, he plucked it, thinking, This is for my agreement. Instantly, the rose in his hand wilted and turned black, and at the same moment, one of the smallest gems on his own great crown grew dull. The King was startled and looked from the blackened flower to his crown, but he knew not what to make of it. Yet, because the Queen remained bright and cheerful, he soon put the strange event from his mind, attributing it to some oddity of nature.
But each time the King felt a moment of pure delight in a beautiful thing and thought to fulfill his promise, another rose would blacken, and another gem would lose its lustre. As weeks turned into months, the King's great crown, once so brilliant, became noticeably dimmer. The King had two sons; the elder, a thoughtful Prince, watched his father and noted the fading light of the crown's gems with a troubled mind. The younger Prince, quiet and observant, also marked the growing shadows in the corners of the grand halls, though he spoke no word of it. The Queen, however, remained untouched by any sorrow, and the King, seeing only her smile, tried to ignore the dimming of his crown. Yet, the flowers in the royal gardens began to lose their fragrance, and the fruit on the trees lacked its former sweetness. The court, too, began to whisper of a change, a dullness in the air, a lack of vibrancy they could not name. Even the kingdom's famed sunshine seemed less warm, and the shadows in the afternoon stretched longer than they once had. The King ordered more festivals and celebrations, hoping to lift the spirits of his people and his own heavy heart. But each attempt to bring forth joy from beautiful things only hastened the dimming of the royal crown's remaining light.
At last, the King could no longer ignore the palpable gloom that had settled over his palace and the crown's dull state. He summoned his wisest counselors, men who had served his father before him, and asked them what afflicted his kingdom. They shook their heads, for they were learned in matters of state and season, but not in shadows of the heart; they spoke of ill omens or natural imbalances. All this while, the Queen continued to smile and sing, her own spirit seemingly untouched by the creeping despair that affected all else. Then the thoughtful elder Prince approached his father and said, Forgive my boldness, Father, but it seems the Queen's renewed joy and the crown's dimming began at the same time. The King looked sharply at his son and said, Speak no such folly; the Queen's happiness is a blessing, not a cause for sorrow. Yet, Father, replied the Prince, remember you not the first rose plucked for the stranger, and the first gem that darkened that very hour? A flicker of unease crossed the King's face, for he remembered that moment well, though he had tried to forget it. It was merely a coincidence, a trick of the light, the King said, waving his hand dismissively, though his voice lacked conviction. Then the quiet younger Prince stepped forward and said softly, Yet shadows deepen when joy is stolen elsewhere, Father.
The King gazed from one son to the other, and the truth of their words, though terrible, began to weigh upon his heart. He recalled the Sorcerer's cold eyes and the smooth words that promised relief for a seemingly small price, and a hidden meaning now shone through. He went to the great window and looked out over his capital city; where once all was bright, now a subdued light fell, and shadows clung to the eaves. And in that moment, he understood the terrible nature of the bargain: his Queen's peace was bought with his kingdom's joy. A great weight settled upon him then, heavier than his crown, and he sank into his throne, his face ashen. At that moment, the Queen entered the throne room, her smile as bright as the summer sun, and asked, My King, why do you look so somber on such a fine day? The King looked at her radiant face, the source of his kingdom's undoing, and could not bring himself to tell her the truth, but forced a smile. But in his heart, he knew he must seek out the Sorcerer again, however perilous the journey, and try to undo the dreadful bargain. For it was whispered in forgotten lore that such a Sorcerer dwelt beyond the desolate shore, across a sea of encroaching darkness. There, upon a crag of black rock, stood a stark, black tower from which no light ever emanated, the supposed home of the dark magic.
The very next dawn, the King rose before the palace stirred, dressed in simple travelling clothes, and spoke to no one of his intent. He left his dimming crown upon its cushion in the treasury and, with a heavy heart, set his face towards the coast. For many days he travelled, and the paths grew wilder and the air grew colder the further he went from his sun-blessed lands. At length, he came to the desolate shore he had heard of in lore, where a grey and restless sea stretched before him, and the only sound was the moan of the wind. An ancient ferryman, with a face like wrinkled parchment and eyes like chips of stone, sat waiting in a small, weathered boat. The King approached and said, Take me to the Black Tower of the Sorcerer across this sea. The ferryman replied, The price is steep for such a crossing. I will pay it, said the King, whatever it may be, for my need is great. The ferryman nodded slowly. Then give me a memory of your brightest joy, one untouched by any shadow, and I will take you. The King paused, then thought of his wedding day, and the Queen's first smile as his bride, a moment of pure, unblemished happiness. He offered it from his heart, and as the ferryman took it, the King felt a part of his own light seem to fade, though he said nothing.
The small boat then moved across the black water under a sky with no stars, and an oppressive silence filled the air. After a time that seemed an age, the Black Tower rose before them, a needle of black glass against the lightless sky. The boat grated on a shingle of jagged black stones, and the King stepped onto the unhallowed ground at the tower's foot. A single door of dark, unyielding metal was set into its base, with no handle or lock visible to the eye. The King called out in a strong voice, Sorcerer, I have come to treat with you concerning the bargain we made! His voice echoed around the bleak crags, but for a long moment, no reply came, only the sighing of the wind. Then, with a sound like stones grinding together, the dark metal door groaned open slowly, revealing only utter darkness within. Though every instinct bade him flee, the King drew his sword, its familiar gleam swallowed by the gloom, and stepped inside the tower. A winding stair, carved from the same black stone as the tower itself, spiraled upwards into the oppressive darkness, lit by a faint, cold light from some unseen source. With his sword held ready, the King began to climb the seemingly endless steps, each one taking him further into the Sorcerer's domain.
As he climbed, the chill in the air intensified, and a faint scent of dust and forgotten magic reached his nostrils. The silence was profound, broken only by the drip of water somewhere in the darkness and, at times, by whispers too faint to understand. Yet the King pressed onward, his mind fixed on the Queen's plight and his kingdom's fading light. At last the stair opened into a vast, circular chamber with a high, domed ceiling, lit only by a single brazier burning with a strange, green flame. And there, seated on a throne of carved black stone, was the cloaked and hooded figure of the Sorcerer, who did not move or speak as the King entered. I have come for my kingdom's joy, which you have stolen through your deceitful bargain! the King declared, his voice steady despite the tower's chill. A dry chuckle, like rustling parchment, came from beneath the hood. Stolen, you say? Or freely given for a price you deemed small? The Sorcerer’s words were soft, yet they carried the weight of ancient, unyielding laws. A bargain is a bargain, King. There must be a way to amend this, the King implored, to release my kingdom from this creeping blight. The hooded head tilted slightly. Perhaps, for everything has its price, and the release from one bargain may be bought with another price, of course.
As he spoke, the green flame in the brazier leapt higher, casting dancing shadows on the stone walls of the circular chamber. Name it, then, the King said, his voice tight, though he felt a cold dread creep into his already heavy heart. The Sorcerer's voice was devoid of all warmth. The brightest joy of one of your own sons, offered freely by him, upon the day he finds it. And this joy, once given to me, will sever the old tie that drains your land, but the son whose joy is taken will know no true happiness again. The King recoiled as if struck. To doom one of his own sons to such a fate was a price too terrible to comprehend. He looked at the unmoving Sorcerer, whose eyes, hidden in shadow, seemed to gleam with cold amusement at his distress. I cannot, the King finally said, his voice choked. I cannot condemn my own child to a life without joy. Then your kingdom will continue to fade, the Sorcerer stated, and your Queen's borrowed peace will be all that remains of its former glory. The King stood trapped between two unbearable choices, the weight of his world resting upon his decision. He turned without another word and slowly descended the dark stairs, the Sorcerer's chill laughter echoing behind him.
He emerged from the dark tower and stood once more upon the barren shore, his shoulders bowed under an invisible weight. The ancient ferryman waited by his boat, his stony eyes showing no change, as if no time had passed since their first meeting. Is your business concluded with the master of the tower? the ferryman asked, his voice like the grating of pebbles. It is far from concluded, the King answered heavily, and a new shadow now resided in his heart as he stepped into the boat. The ferryman asked no more, but plied his oars, and the small boat cut through the black water towards the distant, brighter shore. When they reached the desolate shore of his own kingdom, the King saw a faint glimmer of the familiar sun struggling through the oppressive clouds. He thanked the silent ferryman, who vanished with his boat, and then the King began his long walk inland towards his capital. The land he passed through seemed more barren than when he had left, the colors of even the hardiest plants more muted and dull. He saw fewer people in the fields and villages, and those he met had faces that were drawn, and their eyes held little light. When he finally reached his palace, its once shining walls now appeared tarnished, and the very air around it felt heavy.
His two sons were the first to greet him in the great hall, their young faces grave as they saw his weariness and the new depth of sorrow in his eyes. Father, you have returned, the elder Prince said, his voice low, But what news do you bring from your hard journey? The King looked at his hopeful sons, and the words he had to speak were like stones in his throat. There is a way, he said at last, his voice rough, a way to lift the curse from this land and restore its light. The Princes leaned forward, a spark of anticipation in their eyes, for any hope was welcome in these dimming days. But the price, the King continued, his gaze falling to the stone floor, the price is the brightest, freely given joy of one of my own sons. A stunned silence filled the hall as the Princes tried to comprehend the meaning of their father's words. The elder Prince, ever analytical, was the first to speak, his voice careful. Freely given joy, Father? How could such a thing be ensured or taken? The King explained the Sorcerer's cruel design: that the joy must be genuine, at its peak, and the son unaware that his happiness would be the kingdom's ransom. The quiet younger Prince grew pale, and he looked towards the grand windows, where even now the light seemed to struggle against an encroaching grey.
To sacrifice one for the many, the elder Prince mused, his brow furrowed in thought, It is a terrible calculus often faced by rulers. But to choose which son? the King whispered, his own gaze fixed on his children, each beloved, each a part of his very soul. The Queen, unaware of this dreadful council, entered then, her hand outstretched towards the King, a radiant, perfect lily held within it. My love, see this bloom I found, she said, her voice clear as a spring morning, Is it not the most joyful flower in all our gardens? The King looked at the lily, and a wave of pure, sharp joy at its perfection and his Queen's delight pierced through his despair. He reached for it, intending to fulfill the Sorcerer's due, but before his fingers touched the stem, the lily in her hand blackened and crumbled to dust. At the same instant, three more gems on the King's forgotten crown, resting on its nearby stand, went entirely dark. The Queen cried out, a small, bewildered sound, and looked at the black dust in her hand, then at the King's stricken face. What enchantment is this? she whispered, her eyes wide with sudden fear, a fear that mirrored the one now dawning in her sons' hearts. The King, seeing no escape from the truth any longer, finally told them all of the Sorcerer, the bargain, and the terrible price now demanded for the kingdom's release.
As he spoke, the remaining light from the grand windows seemed to shrink, and the shadows in the hall stretched like grasping hands. The Queen listened, her face losing all its borrowed color, the lily's black dust still staining her fingers. When the King finished, a deep silence fell, heavier than any that had come before, for now understanding was joined with despair. The elder Prince looked at his younger brother, then at his father, his analytical mind grappling with the sheer cruelty of the Sorcerer's new terms. It is a choice that cannot be made, Father, the elder Prince said finally, his voice firm despite the tremor he could not quite conceal. To condemn either of us, or for one to condemn himself for the sake of the kingdom, is to invite a different kind of shadow upon us all. The quiet younger Prince then spoke, his voice surprisingly steady. If joy must be the price, then perhaps joy itself holds the key. All eyes turned to him, for his pronouncements, though rare, often carried a strange and unexpected wisdom. The King looked at his younger son, a flicker of something unreadable in his own shadowed eyes. What mean you, child? Before the Prince could answer, a cold wind swept through the throne room, though all windows were closed, and the last few lights upon the great crown extinguished utterly.
The great crown itself, now a circlet of blackened metal and dull, sightless stones, fell from its stand with a mournful clang. A gasp went around the small royal family, not of surprise, but of a despair too deep for tears. The Queen, seeing the lifeless crown and the terror in her husband's eyes, finally understood the shadow was not merely within her, but had consumed their world. She swayed and would have fallen, but the thoughtful elder Prince caught her arm, his own face a mask of grim resolve. The darkness in the hall was now so profound that their faces were but pale blurs to one another. It is into this complete darkness, the younger Prince said, his voice clear and calm, that a new kind of light must be invited. He spoke not of fighting the Sorcerer with his own dark power, nor of another bargain, but of something originating from within their own afflicted hearts. The King, the Queen, and the elder Prince listened, for in the utter ruin of their former hope, any path that was not the Sorcerer's offered a thread to grasp. Tell us your thoughts, then, my son, the King said, his voice barely a whisper in the suffocating gloom. The young Prince looked towards the blackened crown lying on the floor, then to the sorrowful faces of his family, and began to speak of a forgotten magic, a ritual of pure, selfless joy.
He said that if a joy could be found so pure and given so freely that it expected nothing, not even remembrance, it might weave a light stronger than the Sorcerer's shadow. But such joy, he warned, could not be manufactured for the purpose of breaking the curse, for then it would not be truly selfless. It must arise naturally from a heart that had forgotten its own sorrow in the genuine happiness of another. The elder Prince considered this. A difficult paradox, he murmured, for how can one feel such joy when all around us is this pervasive gloom? And who amongst us, the King added, his voice filled with weariness, still possesses a heart capable of such untainted delight after so much loss? The Queen, who had been listening intently, a new resolve hardening her soft features, then spoke for the first time since hearing the full truth. If my unwitting joy was the conduit for this darkness, she said, her voice trembling but clear, then perhaps my conscious sorrow can help find this selfless light. She proposed that they themselves, the royal family, must be the first to seek such a moment, not for their own sakes, but purely for the love of their dying land. The King and his sons looked at her, and in her eyes, no longer bright with borrowed peace but shining with a fierce, sorrowful love, they found a new, albeit fragile, hope. Thus, it was agreed they would not despair, but watch, and wait, and tend to any small spark of happiness in others, hoping one might ignite the selfless flame.
The days that followed were heavy with this new, quiet vigilance, a shared secret purpose within the palace walls. The King no longer sought roses for the Sorcerer, and the Queen, though her heart ached for her people, made no show of forced cheer. They moved among their diminished court and populace with open eyes, listening more than they spoke, searching for that elusive, unbidden spark. The elder Prince recorded meticulous notes of the fading sunlight, the exact hue of the wilting plants, seeking a pattern or weakness in the curse's hold. The younger Prince, meanwhile, would often sit in the marketplace, not buying or selling, but simply watching the faces of the common folk. He looked for tiny gestures of kindness, a shared crust of bread, a child's brief, forgetful laugh, anything that hinted at a joy untainted by the kingdom's sorrow. But the shadow lay heavy, and such moments were fleeting and rare, quickly swallowed by the overarching weariness that gripped the land. One evening, the Queen sat by her window, looking out at the dimming landscape, her own heart a reflection of its sorrow. She saw two children in the courtyard below, sharing a single, withered apple with such solemn fairness that neither took a larger bite. And in that small, unremarked act of perfect sharing, a tiny, almost imperceptible warmth touched the Queen's own grieving heart.
She did not think of the curse, nor of the Sorcerer, nor even of the kingdom's plight in that instant. Her heart simply felt that small warmth, a genuine response to the children's innocent grace. And as that pure, selfless warmth blossomed within her, a single, tiny gem on the blackened crown on the floor flickered with a faint, hesitant light. The King and his sons, who had been watching the Queen with anxious hope, saw the flicker and held their breath. The light in the gem was no more than a firefly's gleam, and it lasted but a moment before fading again, yet it had been undeniably there. A collective sigh, too soft to be heard, passed between the father and his sons; it was not victory, but it was not utter despair either. The Queen herself had not seen the gem's brief light, for her gaze had been fixed on the memory of the children's goodness. But the King knew, and his sons knew, that a path had been shown, a slender chance woven from an act of innocent sharing. From that day forward, their quiet vigilance took on a new quality, not just of seeking, but of trying to foster such unbidden moments in the beleaguered kingdom. For they understood now that the Sorcerer's power fed on despair and stolen delight, but this new magic, if it could be nurtured, grew from a joy freely and purely given.
Their task was therefore not to make joy, but to make space for joy to perhaps find itself in the hearts of others. The King began by ordering that small, forgotten parks within the city be cleared of weeds and opened for the children to play, with no expectation but their play. The Queen, with her own hands, started tending a small herb garden whose plants were said to soothe sorrow, sharing its leaves freely with any who asked. The elder Prince established quiet places for study and contemplation, hoping that in stillness, some burdened heart might find a moment's unlooked-for peace. The younger Prince continued his walks, now not merely observing, but sometimes leaving a single, perfect fruit where a hungry child might find it, unseen. These were small gestures in a vast ocean of gloom, and for many days, the blackened crown lay unchanged upon the dusty floor of the throne room. The people of the kingdom, though subtly cared for, still moved with downcast eyes, their laughter a forgotten sound. The King watched his Queen, who, though no longer falsely bright, carried her own true sorrow with a quiet dignity that pained his heart anew. He knew the Sorcerer's offer still hung in the air, a terrible temptation to end the suffering, if only he would choose a son. But the memory of the single, faintly glowing gem, and his sons' unified resolve against such a choice, held him to their new, more arduous path.
But the younger Prince looked at the lifeless crown, and then at his mother's tear-streaked but resolute face, and he felt a quiet certainty that this was indeed the only way. One day, the younger Prince returned from the city's edge, his usually calm face lit with a suppressed excitement. I have seen a thing, Father, he said, his voice hushed, A very poor woodcutter, whose only child lay near death from fever. The woodcutter had spent his last coin on a single, small orange for the child, a fruit unseen in these parts for many a long year due to the blight. He offered this treasure to the child, not to cure her, for he knew it could not, but simply to bring her a moment's delight in her suffering. The child, weak as she was, smiled at the bright color and the sweet scent, a true and sudden smile of pure, unthinking joy. And the woodcutter, seeing that smile, forgot his poverty, forgot his fear, and his own heart swelled with a joy so immense it brought tears to his eyes. It was this joy, the Prince explained, his voice filled with awe, this utterly selfless delight in another's happiness, that I believe we have sought. The King and Queen and the elder Prince listened, and for the first time since the crown had fallen, a true and shared hope began to warm the chill of the throne room. They knew not yet how such a private, sacred moment could be brought to bear against the Sorcerer's vast and malevolent power.
